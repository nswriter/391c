YouTube and Closed Captions

YouTube offers four basic ways to add closed captions:

 Upload:

  • Allows you to a closed caption file that already includes timestamps, created in an external software and using a supported file type such as “.sub”
  • Some of these files can encode additional information such as text position and color
Auto-Sync:
  • Allows you to add a transcript which is then auto-synchronized to your video
  • This feature is only available for short videos with only English spoken, and good audio quality
Manually Type:
  • Allows you to manually add a transcript to your video, setting the timings by scrubbing through the video incrementally and pasting the content in where appropriate
Auto-Translate:
  • Auto-Translate is the standard “auto generate closed captions” which many videos have, and is also available for live streaming content.

Naturally, these varying options provide different levels of accuracy and quality. Many content creators will default to auto-translated captions as this offers a largely accurate captioning experience with little to no effort. For some content, such as live streams, this is the only option. However, this solution is imperfect. Everyone, whether they rely on captions or not, will likely find themselves frustrated (or sometimes humored) by glaring errors in the auto translation from time to time. Indeed, YouTube even has a feature to censor potentially inappropriate words in the captions, but not for the reason one might think. This feature isn’t designed with actual inappropriate content from the video in mind (though it does apply to that, too) but rather to conceal errors in the captioning. YouTube knows that its software, while robust, is flawed and can turn even completely wholesome and innocuous content into inappropriate words.

However, for content creators who are willing to put in a little extra effort YouTube’s other captioning options offer much better solutions. These options allow creators to upload custom captions–and, helpfully, multiple sets of captions in different languages if they choose–which stay true to the actual content of their videos. Additionally, these custom captions can be created intentionally with the closed captioning best practices in mind. Also, YouTube accepts a variety of file types for these captions, and some can even include more advanced formatting content such as positioning and color. These extra features can allow the creator even more flexibility to create a rich and immersive captioning experience.

For example, Minecraft YouTuber GeminiTay uses different colored captions (as well as speaker identifiers) to clearly identify who is speaking in her videos. Other YouTubers have also begun to adopt some form of initials or other identifiers in their videos in recent years, but GeminiTay’s captions (named Gem CC in YouTube’s settings) are a loving standard that many would do well to strive towards.

Certainly such detailed captions require extra work, and cost, with many YouTubers choosing to hire additional staff or volunteers to put them together. This commitment to their audience, and especially to those members who require captions, is well worth the effort.

Ultimately, though, YouTube’s varying captioning options have ushered in an era in which captioning is exceptionally accessible to creators, and there is little excuse to not at least toggle on “auto-captioning”.

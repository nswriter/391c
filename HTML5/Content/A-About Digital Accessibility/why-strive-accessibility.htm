Why Strive Towards Digital Accessibility?

Accessibility should be considered a human right, and in many countries is protected by legislation. It has, after a brutally long–and, indeed, ongoing–civil rights battle, become widely accepted that new buildings should be accessible, and old buildings retrofitted wherever possible. Ramps, press-to-open door switches, elevators, and more have all become ubiquitous parts of most civil infrastructure. But what about the web? Web accessibility aims to provide similar infrastructure in the digital landscape. As the Justice Department poses, “inaccessible web content means that people with disabilities are denied equal access to information. An inaccessible website can exclude people just as much as steps at an entrance to a physical location,” (ADA.gov). People deserve access to the web, especially as so many of our daily activities migrate to it. Paying bills, setting up important medical appointments, and more are all tethered to the ability to access websites, and if these websites are not accessible, a large portion of the population may be at a great disadvantage.

Indeed, the CDC suggests that up to 1 in 4 Americans have a disability. What some people might try to sweep away as a small portion of the population is actually much more common than one might think, and working to make a more accessible world and world wide web helps everyone. Accessibility might be hard at times, and it might take some extra work, but as these modules will show, it’s exceedingly attainable and very much worth the effort.
